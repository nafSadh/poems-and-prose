# Second Cup

<date: March 15, 2026 />

The drawing above the counter showed a green sky and she almost left because of
it.

Not because it bothered her. Because she recognized it. She'd drawn it. She was
sure of it — the way the sun had a face, the way the ocean was a single flat
line, the way the figure on the beach had both arms up like they were catching
something falling from above. She'd drawn that figure a hundred times in first
grade, always on the beach, always with the arms up, and her teacher had asked
her once, _what is she catching_, and she'd said _tomorrow_ and the teacher had
laughed and she'd hated the teacher for the rest of the year.

She hadn't thought about that in maybe twenty-five years. And now here it was,
pinned above the espresso machine in a coffee house on South First that she'd
walked into because it was 107 degrees outside and she needed water.

"Excuse me — who drew that?"

The barista didn't look up. "Been there since before me."

"How long have you worked here?"

"Too long."

She took her water and sat under the drawing. It was crayon on manila paper. The
corners were curling. It wasn't signed but in the bottom right, in a child's
handwriting, it said: _for mama when she gets there._

She hadn't written that.

She stared at it and the espresso machine hissed and the hiss became surf and
the dark room became bright and she was on the beach.

---

The heat hit first. Then the light — equatorial, straight down, no shadows
anywhere. White sand that squeaked when she shifted her weight. Behind her, a
low concrete wall with a mural she could feel without turning around, the way
you feel someone standing too close on a bus. She turned around anyway.

The mural was wild — crammed with people, buses, animals, fruit sellers, a man
on a bicycle carrying a door, a woman nursing a baby on the roof of a truck. And
in the middle, bigger than everything else, a tree. Its branches held clocks
instead of fruit and every clock showed a different time and under the tree a
man was sleeping with his mouth open and one arm thrown over his face like the
light was too much even with his eyes closed.

He was snoring. She could hear it over the waves.

"Hey," she said.

Nothing.

"Hey. Where is this?"

He moved his arm half an inch. One eye opened. It was the wrong color — not a
color eyes come in, something between gold and green, the color of old glass
bottles.

"Same place," he said.

"Same as what?"

"Same as where you were."

"I was in a coffee house."

"Yes."

"This isn't a coffee house."

"Also yes." He put his arm back. Eye closed.

She looked down. She was barefoot, which was wrong — she'd been wearing the
sandals she'd bought at the airport in Lisbon three years ago that were falling
apart but she kept wearing because she'd been happy in Lisbon. She could see her
toes in the white sand and they looked like her toes but also slightly not her
toes, the way your hands look in a dream, correct but unsigned.

A dog came down the beach carrying a shoe in its mouth. A child's sandal, red,
buckled. The dog dropped it at her feet and looked at her with the unbearable
sincerity of dogs.

"That's not mine," she told the dog.

The dog disagreed.

She picked it up. It was warm. Not sun-warm. People-warm. Like someone had just
taken it off. She turned it over and on the sole, in faded marker, someone had
written a phone number. She memorized it without deciding to. The dog sat down,
satisfied.

She looked back at the mural. The sleeping man under the clock tree was gone.
The tree was there, the clocks were there, the indentation in the painted grass
where he'd been lying was there. But he was gone.

She looked at the actual man sleeping under the actual tree beside the wall. He
was watching her now, both eyes open, wrong-colored and unblinking.

"You're in the painting," she said.

"I was in the painting. I heard you coming."

"You can't hear someone coming from inside a painting."

"You can't be on a beach from inside a coffee house." He sat up. He was very old
and very thin and his white shirt had paint on it — the same blue as the mural's
ocean. "You drew me," he said.

"I didn't."

"Green sky. One line for the ocean. Figure with her arms up, catching tomorrow.
You drew that when you were six. You drew me into the tree and I've been here
since." He said this without accusation, the way someone tells you what they had
for breakfast. "It's been a while."

"That's not how drawings work."

"How would you know? You stopped." He stood. He was tall. The clocks on the
painted tree behind him were moving now — she was sure of it, the minute hands
creeping in different directions, some forward, some backward, one spinning fast
enough to blur. "You drew the green sky because you could see it. Your teacher
laughed and you stopped seeing it. That's the whole problem, the whole thing,
right there. Someone laughs and the sky goes back to blue."

The espresso machine hissed.

---

She was in the coffee house. Her water had tipped over and was dripping off the
table onto her lap and the barista was coming over with a rag and saying
something she couldn't hear because her ears were full of surf.

The phone number. She still had it. She pulled out her phone and typed it in and
stared at it. A 212 number. New York.

She called it.

It rang four times. A girl picked up — young, maybe seven or eight.

"Hello?"

"Hi. I — someone gave me this number."

"I know. Are you at the beach?"

A cold feeling. Not fear exactly. The feeling of a large thing moving slowly in
deep water beneath you.

"I was. I think."

"I drew you a map but I put the sky wrong. I used green because I ran out of
blue. But actually it IS green there, so it worked out." The girl's voice was
bright and practical, the voice of someone explaining a completely reasonable
thing. "The man under the tree says you're coming but you keep going back to the
coffee place."

"Who are you?"

"You know who I am."

She did. She did and the knowing was a door opening onto a room she'd been
standing outside of for thirty-one years, since first grade, since the green
sky, since _what is she catching_ and the answer that made her teacher laugh and
the laughter that had shut something in her so firmly she'd mistaken the lock
for a wall.

"The drawing says _for mama when she gets there_," she said.

"Yeah."

"I'm not —"

"Not yet. That's what the map is for."

The barista was mopping up the water. The pendant lamps were amber. Package
tracking, logistics, the arrow of time from origin to destination — she could
feel all of that, the ordinary sequence of the ordinary world, holding its shape
around her like scaffolding. And under it, beneath it, the surf. The dog. The
sleeping man who'd waited in a painting for thirty-one years because a
six-year-old drew him there and then forgot.

"The sky is green," she told the barista.

"Whatever you say."

She looked at the drawing. The figure on the beach with both arms raised,
catching tomorrow. She'd drawn that. A child she hadn't had yet had written _for
mama when she gets there_. The phone number on the sandal connected to a girl in
New York who knew about the map. The clocks on the tree all showed different
times and they were all correct.

She finished her water. She walked out into 107 degrees. The sky was blue
because that's what skies do when people are watching.

But she wasn't people-watching anymore. She was looking with the wrong eyes,
which is to say the right ones, and just for a second, between the Starbucks and
the parking garage, the sky flickered green.
